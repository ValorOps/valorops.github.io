From MSP Ticket Queue to Enterprise SOC Recognition

April 17, 2026 7 min read

Two malware investigations I ran this year produced findings that were escalated to the head of an enterprise SOC for cross-customer threat hunting awareness. Here's what the process looked like, what it taught me, and where it's pointing next.

The Short Version

Over the last couple of weeks I worked two separate malware incidents in back-to-back windows. The first one started with EDR alerts that an MDR partner had initially evaluated and set aside. The second was from a non-MDR customer in which I had to do 100% of the threat hunting myself. Eventually that second case ended up becoming extremely relative to the first.

The artifacts, reports, and logs from those investigations were sent over to the MDR partner's Advisory Practice Group and SOC for awareness and threat-hunting context across their broader customer base. The findings were significant enough that they were escalated directly to the head of the SOC department.

I'm not going to pretend that's a routine outcome for someone at my level. Technical support engineers at MSPs usually don't end up with their names attached to evidence that makes it onto an enterprise SOC director's desk. It happened because of a specific approach to these cases, and I think that approach is worth writing about.

The full technical write-ups for each case are already published here. This post is the synthesis; what the pattern looked like across both investigations, what I learned, and what it changed about how I work.

What Made These Cases Different

The thing I want to be clear about up front: I didn't find these because I'm special or because I have tools nobody else has. I found them because I treat "benign" determinations as hypotheses, not conclusions.

In both cases, the first read from an external party was some version of "nothing to worry about". False positive, PUA, tune the rule, close the case. That's a reasonable default. Most alerts really are noise, and analysts at scale have to triage fast. But defaults break on edge cases, and edge cases are exactly where the interesting malware lives.

What I did that mattered was slow down long enough to ask a different question. Not "is this alert real?" but "what would I expect to see in the telemetry if this alert were real, and is that there?" Once you frame it that way, the evidence either shows up or it doesn't. And in both of these cases, it showed up clearly once I looked for it specifically.

The Repeatable Part

Two cases is a pattern, not a coincidence. The shape of both investigations was nearly identical, and that's the part I think generalizes.

Lead with timeline, not with the binary. In both investigations, the single most valuable thing I produced wasn't a reverse-engineered IOC, it was a minute-by-minute timeline of what the endpoint actually did. Timelines are what let you say "this process was still executing file operations four hours after the EDR reported mitigation complete." That sentence is unarguable. It ends disputes.

Use the full telemetry layer, not just the alert. EDR alerts are a summary. The full data lake behind them has the story. Querying by storyline or process chain instead of just by filename turned both investigations from "suspicious file was quarantined" into "here's every network connection, registry change, and process spawn this infection produced over the entire infection window." The alert was the starting line. The data lake was the actual investigation.

Detonate when the live evidence has gaps. Both cases had blind spots in live telemetry: firewall logs I couldn't pull, processes too short-lived to capture, C2 behavior I couldn't observe without reconnecting the machine. A controlled sandbox fills those gaps. I used Joe Sandbox in both cases and got the ransom note behavior, the process injection confirmation, the C2 protocol, and the decoy redirect that I couldn't have gotten any other way.

Distinguish what you've confirmed from what you've inferred. This is the one that actually wins disputes. When I wrote these findings up, I was careful to separate "SDL telemetry shows process X doing Y at timestamp Z" from "which suggests the attacker intended to Z." Confirmed facts are load-bearing. Inferences are context. Mixing them up is how credibility evaporates.

Cross-reference everything. Behavioral EDR verdicts, static AV scans, sandbox verdicts, and third-party anti-ransomware platforms are all measuring different things. When you can stack four or five independent sources pointing the same direction, the case is essentially closed. When they disagree, the disagreement itself is the evidence.

What the Escalation Actually Means

I want to be careful not to oversell this part. The MDR partner didn't send a marching band. What happened was a meeting where the cases were discussed as part of a broader conversation about how the partner and the MSP could collaborate more effectively on situations where the MSP-side analyst thinks something isn't benign after the MDR has reverse-engineered it. The malware artifacts, reports, and logs from my investigations were sent over for awareness and threat-hunting context, and the prior case was reopened and raised to the head of SOC.

That's not a Hollywood ending, but it's the real one, and it matters for a reason that isn't obvious: it's the first time I've seen my technical work move up the chain instead of sideways. Most tickets I close stay closed. They serve the customer, and then they disappear into PSA history. These two cases fed into something larger: cross-customer awareness at an enterprise SOC. And that changes what "a good day at work" can look like.

The Honest Part

I've been working toward security-specific work for a while. I run the vulnerability assessment program for one of our largest customers, I've built out a home lab with Proxmox and Wazuh to get more hands-on detection engineering time, and I write about what I learn here so the reasoning is visible and auditable. None of that guarantees anything. But these two cases told me the pattern is working and that if I keep leaning into evidence-driven investigation and keep sharing the work openly, the door to full-time security work opens further every time.

I don't consider myself a cyber-security professional yet. What I hope people understand is that I didn't come across these cases with a wealth of knowledge of how malware works. It's A LOT of looking for what little I know, researching the rest, and filtering out the information until I can come to a conclusion.

The thing I'd tell anyone else in an MSP Tier 2/3 seat who wants to move in this direction: the work is already in your queue. The alerts are already hitting your inbox. The difference between "closed the ticket" and "escalated findings to a SOC director" is almost entirely about how much time you're willing to spend on the cases where your gut says the first read was wrong.

What's next. I'm continuing to document significant incidents here, building out detection engineering practice in the home lab, and expanding the vulnerability assessment program into more mature quarterly cycles. If any of this resonates and you'd like to talk shop, the contact link in the nav works.

Conclusion

I'm proud of these two cases. Not because they were the hardest investigations anyone has ever run (they weren't) but because they show that careful, evidence-led work from an MSP support seat can produce findings that matter at an enterprise level. That's a ceiling a lot of people assume doesn't lift. It does.

The next case will show up sooner than later. When it does, I'll do the same thing: pull the timeline, leverage the tools I know of, detonate what needs detonating, separate confirmed from inferred, and cross-reference until the story either holds together or falls apart. And I'll write it up when it's over.
